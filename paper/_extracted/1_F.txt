ABSTRACT
Real-time collected microscopic images of harmful algal blooms (HABs) in coastal areas often suffer from sig-
nificant color deviations and loss of fine cellular details. To address these issues, this paper proposes an inno-
vative method for enhancing underwater marine algal microscopic images based on Adaptive White Balance
Color Compensation (AWBCC) and Image Pyramid Fusion (IPF). Firstly, an effective Algorithm Adaptive Cyclic
Channel Compensation (ACCC) is proposed based on the gray world assumption to enhance the color of un-
derwater images. Then, the Maximum Color Channel Attention Guidance (MCCAG) method is employed to
reduce color disturbance caused by ignoring light absorption. This paper introduces an Empirical Contrast
Enhancement (ECH) module based on multi-scale IPF tailored for underwater microscopic images of algae, which
is used for global contrast enhancement, texture detail enhancement, and noise control. Secondly, this paper
proposes a network based on a diffusion probability model for edge detection in HABs, which simultaneously
considers both high-order and low-order features extracted from images. This approach enriches the semantic
information of the feature maps and enhances edge detection accuracy. This edge detection method achieves an
ODS of 0.623 and an OIS of 0.683. Experimental evaluations demonstrate that our underwater algae microscopic
image enhancement method amplifies local texture features while preserving the original image structure. This
significantly improves the accuracy of edge detection and key point matching. Compared to several state-of-the-
art underwater image enhancement methods, our approach achieves the highest values in contrast, average
gradient, entropy, and Enhancement Measure Estimation (EME), and also delivers competitive results in terms of
image noise control..
1. Introduction
With the continuous development of underwater image and video
data acquisition technology [ 1 ], improving the quality of underwater
images has become a key issue in underwater machine vision research
and practical applications [ 2 ]. Due to the unique characteristics of the
underwater environment [ 3 , 4 ], such as light refraction, impurity scat-
tering, color distortion, and reduced contrast, underwater images are
often subject to various interferences, resulting in loss of visual infor-
mation [ 5 ]. In order to address these issues, this study proposes an
innovative underwater image enhancement method aimed at improving
the quality of underwater microscopic images [ 6 ] through color channel
compensation [ 7 ] and image enhancement techniques [ 8 ]. This paper
not only provides a detailed introduction to the theoretical foundation
and implementation process of the method but also conducts a series of
experiments to compare it with other advanced techniques, validating
its superiority in color correction, detail preservation, and noise control.
Moreover, by quantitatively analyzing the impact of this method on the
edge detection [ 9 ] results of underwater algal microscopic images, this
paper further demonstrates its ability to significantly enhance the local
detail features of the images while preserving their original structure,
providing crucial technical support for subsequent research on seg-
mentation, identification, and classification of algal microscopic images
in red tide studies.

2. Related work
Preprocessing techniques for underwater images mainly involve
physical methods for image restoration and non-physical methods for
enhancing image quality, namely underwater image restoration and
underwater image enhancement.
2.1. Current research on underwater image enhancement and restoration
The primary objective of underwater image restoration methods is to
improve visibility by deriving a degradation model for underwater im-
ages. Wu et al. proposed a two-stage progressive enhancement network
(TPENet) [10] based on an encoder-decoder architecture to address
color distortion in underwater images. Li et al. addressed the limitations
of the classical dark channel prior algorithm in underwater image
restoration by proposing a novel algorithm based on improved back-
ground light estimation and automatic white balance [11]. Guan et al.
achieved the restoration of degraded underwater images through their
UIE method (DiffWater), which is based on a conditional denoising
diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM) [12]. Alamsyah et al. proposed a
color restoration method for underwater images based on polynomial
equations and fog removal techniques to address the primary factors
distorting underwater images, namely color shifting and light scattering
[13]. These restoration methods often require complex physical models
and prior information, and they are computationally intensive and lack
generalizability.
Underwater image enhancement methods aim to improve image
quality by modifying pixel values and distributions. Liu et al. effectively
enhanced the quality of turbid underwater images with non-uniform
scattering using a pulse-coupled neural network (PCNN) and poly-
nomial regression algorithm [14]. Yu et al. proposed a UIE method
based on the maximum attenuation prior in the red channel, achieving
enhancement for different underwater scene images [15]. Zhang et al.
implemented the enhancement of degraded red tide microscopic images
through homomorphic filtering, empirical mode feature extraction, and
wavelet fusion [16]. Additionally, Chiang and Chen proposed a novel
system approach that considers the influence of possible artificial light
sources and enhances underwater images using a dehazing algorithm
[17]. Although the enhancement/restoration methods have shown
certain effectiveness for underwater images, the degradation factors for
algae underwater microscopic images are different from general un-
derwater images. Therefore, these methods may not be suitable for
effectively enhancing algae underwater microscopic images.
2.2. Current research on microscopic images of HABs
Researchers have proposed various algorithms for enhancing HABs
images. Wu et al. [16] introduced a HABs image enhancement method
that combines empirical mode decomposition (EMD) and discrete
wavelet transform. This approach highlights high-frequency informa-
tion such as textures, hair, and edges by enhancing the high-pass filter.
In terms of HABs image classification, Wu et al. [36] developed a
Weighted Feature Fusion Dual Attention Convolutional Neural Network
and Transformer Encoder Module (WFTC) specifically for marine HABs
image classification. Through the design of an image enhancement al-
gorithm [20] and the HABs classification network (WFTC), this method
significantly improved image quality and achieved a maximum classi-
fication accuracy of 87%. In the field of underwater image enhance-
ment/restoration, Wu et al. [37] proposed a HABs edge detection
method based on a Spatial Feature Mapping Encoder-Decoder Network,
termed SFMnet. This method first enhances the quality of degraded
images in the original HABs dataset by designing an image enhancement
algorithm based on an underwater image restoration model, which in-
cludes precise underwater scene depth estimation and transmission map
fusion to effectively eliminate color distortion and enhance image edge
details and contrast. Additionally, they introduced a deep learning
model based on the VGG16 network, which integrates multi-scale fea-
tures through a Spatial Feature Mapping (SFM) module to improve edge
detection accuracy.
3. Proposed method
The flowchart of our method is detailed in Fig. 1. Our method con-
sists of two main parts: adaptive white balance for underwater algae
microscopic images and image pyramid fusion.
In the first step, we perform channel compensation and balance in
the RGB color space. The three channels are divided into the following
types: large, medium, and small scales. The three channels are divided
into large, medium, and small scales. The channels are divided into the
following types: large, medium, and small. The large channel, with
minimal color attenuation, is compensated using linear operations based
on the maximum pixel values of the medium and small channels. Sub-
sequently, the medium and small channels are cyclically compensated
based on a channel loss function until the process is completed. Next, we
select the image with the maximum color channel attenuation as the
guidance image for color transfer. This approach effectively eliminates
color cast but does not significantly improve contrast or address local
detail blurring.
In the second step, in the image pyramid fusion, we employ an
improved Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE)
and contrast stretching to enhance the global contrast of the image. We
also utilize Empirical Mode Decomposition (EMD [18]) to enrich local
details, effectively addressing the issue of local detail blurring. Through
these steps, we generate an enhanced underwater image.

The colors and contrast of real-time captured underwater algae
microscopic images are prone to degradation. Recent studies [19] have
demonstrated the capability of underwater image color correction.
However, these methods often introduce artifacts or unsatisfactory
features in the context of underwater algae microscopic images, which
hinders the recognition, extraction, and classification of algae features.
In order to better utilize existing images for downstream tasks, we

3.2. Adaptive Cyclic Channel compensation(ACCC) and attenuation map
transfer
Color deviation in underwater images is caused by the selective ab-
sorption of light in the underwater environment [ 21 ]. We reference
existing white balance methods, which typically estimate the color of
the light source by making specific assumptions [ 22 ] and then correct
the colors by multiplying each channel with the corresponding
compensation coefficients. Among these methods, the Gray World [ 23 ]
algorithm is based on the assumption that, for an image with significant
color variations, the average values of the R, G, and B components tend
to the same gray value (Gray). In other words, the Gray World
assumption posits that the average reflectance of objects in the natural
environment is “ gray ” in a global sense. By this assumption, the algo-
rithm calculates the mean values of the RGB channels and attempts to
eliminate the influence of ambient light. However, these methods often
struggle to adaptively restore color deviations in complex and diverse
underwater images.
In our proposed method of adaptive cyclic channel compensation,
underwater algae microscopic images are transformed into three color
channels: red, green, and blue. Firstly, the average gray values of the
red, green, and blue channels are computed, denoted as R_avg, G_avg,
and B_avg, respectively. The channel evaluation values for the red,
green, and blue channels are calculated as follows:

3.3. ECH based on multi-scale image pyramid fusion
In this section, we propose a solution for enhancing the details of
single-frame underwater algal microscopic images based on image
pyramid fusion. Image fusion has been widely applied in image
enhancement and has demonstrated its practicality. We introduce a
method that achieves better enhancement of edge and texture features in
various underwater algal microscopic images. Specifically, our innova-
tion in the ECH approach lies in using the results of empirical mode
decomposition, contrast stretching, and improved CLAHE [ 25 ] as input
components for the pyramid fusion method. Our image detail
enhancement method consists of two steps: obtaining a white-balanced
image with color channel compensation from the original image, and
performing pyramid fusion [ 26 ] with input component images derived
from the white-balanced image.
3.3.1. Empirical mode decomposition (EMD)
Empirical Mode Decomposition (EMD) is a signal decomposition
method based on the time-scale characteristics of the data itself. It does
not require predefined basis functions and is a time-frequency domain
signal processing approach. EMD is particularly suitable for analyzing
nonlinear and non-stationary signals.
In the field of image processing, Empirical Mode Decomposition
(EMD) can be utilized for feature extraction and edge preservation. EMD
decomposes image signals into a series of Intrinsic Mode Functions
(IMFs), which encompass various frequency components of the image.
This approach effectively suppresses noise while preserving edge in-
formation. Specifically, it can be implemented through the extraction of
local extrema, selection of appropriate interpolation methods, and
management of endpoint effects.
1. During the decomposition of image signals using the EMD method,
local extrema, including maxima and minima, are extracted. These
extrema are crucial for capturing edge information in images. By
employing the neighborhood comparison method, local extrema can
be displayed, thereby preserving edge information throughout the
decomposition process.
2. In the EMD decomposition process, the use of surface fitting and
interpolation methods transforms operator-detected edge images
into probability distributions, which are then inversely mapped back
to the range of edge intensity. This maximizes the retention of feature
maps in both IMF images and residual images.
3. The method based on boundary local feature extension involves
determining whether the endpoints of the original signal can be
inserted as extrema into new upper and lower envelopes. This
effectively suppresses the generation of false components and
distortion of boundary signals caused by endpoint effects in EMD.
In our work, we propose a method for extracting empirical mode
feature maps from algal images based on the EMD method. EMD can
separate the high-frequency components in the image, resulting in

high computational complexity due to the need for decomposition along
each dimension of the image. This may result in a significant compu-

prominence of specific details. Additionally, EMD is primarily applied to
grayscale values or individual color channels; processing directly in the
RGB space may cause an imbalance or distortion of color information.

techniques, such as histogram equalization or adaptive contrast
enhancement, to complement the deficiencies of EMD in image pro-
cessing. The second and third input images aim to enhance image
contrast and augment the difference between foreground and back-

4. Experiments results and analysis
In this section, the experimental results generated from the various
stages of the proposed methods are compared with the latest method-
ologies, and cutting-edge evaluation metrics are employed for qualita-
tive and quantitative assessment of the enhanced images. Given our
enhancement targets underwater algae microscopic imaging for further
downstream tasks like classification and edge detection, advanced edge
detection techniques have been applied to evaluate the effectiveness of
our image enhancement approach.
4.1. Dataset description
This section summarizes the datasets used, experimental setup,
experimental results, and provides a brief analysis of the network ar-
chitecture ’ s performance. Currently, there is no publicly available HABs
dataset; therefore, the HABs micrograph dataset utilized in this study
was obtained from the Applied Microalgae Biology Laboratory at Ocean
University of China. This dataset comprises 36 HABs categories, totaling
676 images. For evaluation purposes, the images for each HABs category
were divided into training and testing sets at a 7:3 ratio, with the
training set containing 473 images and the validation set comprising
203 images.
4.2. Experimental setup
To verify the effectiveness of the proposed underwater image
enhancement method, experiments related to enhancement were con-
ducted on a high-performance computer equipped with an Intel Core i5-
12400F CPU and 32 GB of memory. The proposed method is based on
Python 3.6 implementation and utilizes libraries such as NumPy,
OpenCV, and SciPy for efficient image processing and numerical
calculations.
Our edge detection experiments were conducted using the PyTorch
framework with Python 3.6 and PyTorch 1.7.1. For training the
denoising U-Net, the minimum time step was set to 0.0001. The model
was trained using the AdamW optimizer with a decayed learning rate
ranging from 5e-5 to 5e-6 over 5000 iterations. All training was per-
formed on a single RTX 3090 GPU, taking approximately 15 GPU hours
per session. We employed exponential moving average (EMA) to miti-
gate instability in model performance during training. For all experi-
ments, we trained on datasets using randomly cropped patches of size
320 × 320, with a batch size of 16. To ensure fair evaluation, we utilized
identical metrics to assess the edge prediction maps generated by each
method, including Average Precision (AP) [ 39 ], Optimal Dataset Scale
(ODS), and Optimal Image Scale (OIS) F-measure. The generated edges
were not refined during evaluation.

gray, green, purple) demonstrate good performance after our channel
compensation and white balance method. As seen in Fig. 9, the Shades of

color biases, while the Max RGB method amplifies the grayscale values,

highest values for nearly all images on the PCC index, and the lowest

has smaller color differences, effective channel compensation, and
minimal loss of image detail, aligning closely with the human eye’s
actual perception of color discrepancies. Compared to the other two

compensation approach produces images that are closer to the original
in terms of color deviation correction while better distinguishing be-

formance on individual metrics. However, when considering the overall
visual effect and the average values across various metrics, our method
proves to be superior. For the HABs dataset, our channel-compensated
white balance method demonstrates effective correction for algal im-
ages exhibiting different color casts. Our approach exhibits greater
generalization capability compared to other methods. In conclusion,
both qualitative and quantitative comparisons indicate that our method
possesses superior color correction capabilities.

handling algal flagella and complex details, indicating constraints in
detecting extremely fine structures. C-Net utilizes convolutional neural
networks to extract image features, showcasing strong edge detection
capabilities. However, C-Net is prone to missing detections when iden-
tifying small algal flagella. LDCnet enhances edge detection perfor-
mance through intermediate edge map prediction and context-aware
fusion blocks but often encounters challenges with blurred edges in
complex scenes, affecting detection accuracy. There remains room for
improvement with complex backgrounds and weak edges. Our model
significantly improves edge detection accuracy, particularly excelling in
handling complex backgrounds and noise interference.
To validate the effectiveness of HDEnet, we conducted a comparison
on our dataset with both traditional methods and several recent deep
learning-based approaches. This evaluation included classical edge
detection algorithms such as Canny, as well as modern deep learning-
based algorithms, including BDCN, PIDNet, and LDCnet. The compari-
sons in terms of ODS [ 36 ], OIS [ 38 ], and AP are presented in Table 6 .
Our method achieved an F-measure ODS of 0.623, outperforming
several recently proposed methods by 7.7% over BDCN, 9.8% over
PIDNet, and 13.6% over LDCnet.
The experimental results presented above clearly demonstrate the
significant advantages of the proposed HDEnet in the context of harmful
algae edge detection. These findings underscore the efficacy of our
network architecture design. As shown in Table 6 and Fig. 14 , HDEnet
achieved outstanding scores of 0.623 and 0.683 for ODS and OIS,
respectively. It not only surpasses traditional edge detection algorithms
like the Canny operator but also outperforms other deep learning-based
methods such as BDCN, PIDNET, and C-Net. Our network effectively
extracts multi-scale features from images through a meticulously
designed multi-scale encoder-decoder structure. This architecture pre-
serves local detail information while capturing global contextual infor-
mation, which is critical for accurately identifying and delineating the
edges of algae cells. Furthermore, the integration of the HAL module
enhances the network ’ s sensitivity to multi-order features, allowing it to
focus more intently on significant feature information. This enhance-
ment results in a more efficient training process and more accurate edge
detection outcomes.
The comparative results presented in Fig. 14 and Table 6 clearly
demonstrate that our method surpasses other state-of-the-art edge
detection techniques in terms of accuracy, robustness, and the ability to
handle complex scenarios. This not only validates the practical

5. Conclusion
In this study, we propose a novel underwater image processing
method aimed at improving the quality of underwater microscopic im-
ages of algae in seawater through color channel compensation and
image enhancement techniques. Our method is carefully designed to
address the common issues of low contrast, color deviation, and blurred
texture features in underwater images.
Through extensive experimental evaluations, including application
to underwater algal microscopic images with various color tones, our
method demonstrates significant advantages in color correction, pres-
ervation of image details, and noise control. Furthermore, we further
validate that the proposed image enhancement algorithm effectively
enhances the visual quality of underwater algal microscopic images.
This enhancement not only significantly improves the local details and
texture features of the images but also preserves the original structure
and purity of the images. The results of key point matching and edge
detection further demonstrate the effectiveness of our method in
improving the quality of underwater microscopic images. By utilizing
edge detection, we are able to identify the morphological features of
algal cells more clearly, which is of great significance for scientific
research and applications.

scopic image enhancement method based on adaptive white balance
