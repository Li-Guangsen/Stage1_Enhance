Underwater enhancement computing of ocean HABs based 
on cyclic color compensation and multi-scale fusion
Geng‑Kun Wu1,2  · Jie Xu1 · Yi‑Dan Zhang1 · Bei‑Ping Zhang1
Received: 23 November 2022 / Revised: 25 April 2023 / Accepted: 4 July 2023 
© The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer Science+Business Media, LLC, part of Springer Nature 2023
Abstract
The clarity of algal images is crucial for solving problems such as harmful algal blooms 
(HABs) classification and red tide warning. However, the original underwater microscopic 
images of HABs often produce color distortion and blurred details due to the effects of sea-
water quality, illumination and image extraction techniques. To deal with the above prob-
lems, an enhancement computing method based on cyclic color compensation and three-
image Multi-scale fusion is proposed in this paper. In this method, first, we propose a novel 
cyclic color compensation method to correct the color of the microscopic images of HABs. 
Then, with the input of three images derived from the color compensation versions of the 
original images of the algae, the correlation weight maps of the three inputs are redefined 
to facilitate the transfer of texture features and color contrast to the output images. Finally, 
to avoid image artifacts in the low-frequency components of the output image after the 
weight map transformation, a novel image Multi-scale fusion strategy is used in this paper. 
Qualitative and quantitative experimental results show that the underwater microscopic 
images of HABs enhanced with the method proposed in this paper have the best texture 
clarity and global contrast, and the method significantly improves the accuracy of transmis-
sion map estimation, edge detection and key point matching in processing algal images.
Keywords Harmful algal blooms · Enhancement computing · Cyclic color compensation · 
Multi-scale fusion

2  Related work
This section focuses on the current research in underwater image enhancement/restora-
tion. The methods for underwater image processing are classified into underwater image 
enhancement (IFM-FREE) and underwater image restoration (IFM) based on whether they 
are based on an image formation model (IFM) or not [32].
2.1  Underwater image recovery
Recovering underwater images by building an underwater imaging model, calculating 
the model parameters, and solving the underwater imaging model inverse to recover the 
underwater images is called underwater image recovery [34]. Lu et al. [27] proposed an 
adaptive underwater image recovery method based on Multi-scale cyclic generative adver -
sarial network (MCycleGAN) and dark channel prior (DCP). In Lu′s method, the contrast 
of the image is improved by using the multi-scale structural similarity index measurement 
(SSIM) loss. Li et al. [22] proposed a Retinex model with robustness, which additionally 
considers the noise figure to enhance the quality of low-light images compared to the con-
ventional Retinex model. The method can also be used for remote sensing applications and 
in hazy and dusty environmental conditions. Emberton et al. [9] proposed a novel defog-
ging method that improves the quality of underwater images by segmenting the image 
region containing only water. A semantic white balance method for light source estimation 
was also proposed, which was used to address the spectral distortion present in underwater 
scenes. Li et al. [23] proposed a two-stage underwater image defogging method. The first 
stage removes fine dust using underwater red and green minimum channel a priori de-scat-
tering, and the second stage uses a deep convolutional neural network to further remove

sate for the most lost red channel and thus improve the image contrast. Akkaynak et al. [4] 
used RGB images to directly obtain the shooting distance d for each pixel, which was then 
brought into an underwater imaging model to obtain a recovered underwater image.
2.2  Underwater image enhancement
The direct application of graphic image processing techniques to enhance underwater 
images without considering specific underwater imaging models was called underwater 
image enhancement. Underwater image enhancement methods can be further divided into 
two main approaches: hardware-based and software-based methods. The software-based 
methods can be further divided into three categories: contrast enhancement, color correc-
tion, and hybrid methods. Hybrid methods combine contrast enhancement, defogging, and 
color correction [41].
Hardware-based There are four main hardware-based methods for underwater image 
enhancement, namely, polarization, distance-gated imaging, fluorescence imaging, and 
stereo imaging [26]. However, a disadvantage of hardware-based enhancement methods is 
that they are very expensive and relatively power-consuming.
Software-based Arpit et al. [26] proposed a deep learning architecture consisting of an 
integrated encoder and decoder to correct the color of underwater images. This approach 
solves the underwater image color correction problem as a coloring task. To improve the 
image contrast and correct the color bias of underwater images, Huo et al. [16] proposed an 
underwater image enhancement algorithm based on a deep learning network and a wavelet 
enhancement learning strategy (PRWNet). The method uses a multi-stage refinement strat-
egy to effectively enhance the degradation of spatial variations in a coarse-to-fine manner. 
The image is enhanced from the low and high frequency components of the image, respec-
tively. Luchman et  al. [28] proposed a dark channel prior (ADCP) adaptive defogging 
algorithm combined with image enhancement. Shi et al. [37] designed an encoder-aggre-
gator-decoder architecture for underwater image enhancement. And a multiscale feature 
transformation module was introduced to transform the extracted multiscale semantic-level 
features to improve the robustness of the encoder-aggregator-decoder network. Azmi et al. 
[7] proposed a nature-based underwater image color enhancement (NUCE) method that 
can significantly correct chromatic aberrations. A population intelligence-based equali-
zation algorithm was also proposed to improve the naturalness of the output image, and 
finally an inverse sharpening mask was applied to improve the sharpness of the image. 
Li et  al. [24] proposed an underwater image enhancement network (Ucolor) with multi-
color space embedding guided by media transport. This method takes advantage of multi-
ple color space embedding as well as physical model-based and learning-based approaches 
to improve the visual quality of underwater images. Gao et al. [12] proposed an underwater 
image enhancement model inspired by the morphology and function of the scleractinian 
retina. The method can enhance the edge and contrast of underwater images well.
Although the above enhancement/restoration methods are effective for underwater 
images, there are still some differences between the degradation causes of underwater 
microscopic images of HABs and general underwater images, so the above methods are not 
well suited for underwater microscopic images of HABs.

3  Proposed method
3.1  Cyclic color compensation
When taking underwater microscopic images of HABs, some images will be affected by 
seawater quality, lighting, and other factors leading to degradation in quality, a critical 
manifestation of which is the deviation of the image color. To correct the color deviation of 
the image, we propose a novel cyclic color compensation method to process the image. The 
purpose of cyclic color compensation is to compensate for the loss of color channels and to 
correct the hue of the image.
We refer to the most currently available white balance methods, most of which estimate 
the color of a light source by making specific assumptions and then multiply each color 
channel by the corresponding compensation factor to achieve color correction. In these 
methods, Gray World algorithms assume that the mean value of the average reflection of 
natural scenery to light is a constant value in general. Therefore, the Gray World algorithm 
estimates the color distribution of the light source by averaging the pixel values of each 
channel independently. Max RGB assumes that the maximum response of the RGB color 
channels in the image is caused by the white surfaces in the scene. Therefore, the larg-
est value in the RGB channel will be used as the light color of the image. To make Gray 
World more general, Finalayson et  al. introduced the Minkowski paradigm (Minkowski 
norm) based on Gray World and proposed the Shades of Gray algorithm. This algorithm 
uses the Minkowski norm distance instead of the simple averaging method. Weijer et al. 
propose a new Gray Edge hypothesis: the difference in the average reflection of all physical 
surfaces in the scene is colorless. Also, to be compatible with several basic algorithms, the 
Minkowski paradigm was introduced to obtain a more general algorithmic framework for 
calculating color constancy.
In this paper, we propose a novel cyclic color compensation strategy that is effective and 
suitable for solving the chromatic aberration problem of HABs microscopic images. We 
will explain the method in detail in the following.
In the method of this paper, the underwater microscopic images of greenish HABs are 
used as an example. We based the following five observations on compensating for the loss 
of the red and blue channels.
(1) The greenish HABs images have lower average grey values for the red and blue chan-
nels, while the green channel has the highest average grey value.
(2) The green channel contains the color information that is lost in the red and blue chan-
nels. Because of this, we compensate for the red and blue channels by using the green 
channel.
(3) After a lot of experiments, we found that the compensation of the red channel should 
be proportional to the difference between the average grey value of the green channel 
and the red channel. The same is true for the compensation of the blue channel. This 
difference in average value reflects the degree of attenuation of the channel.
(4) To avoid the artifacts in the HABs image, the red and blue channels should be com-
pensated in places where the attenuation is high, and compensation is not needed for 
areas where the red and blue channel values are already high.
(5) Since the attenuation degree of the red and blue channels of HABs images is different, 
we cannot find the suitable parameters to make all images achieve the best effect in 
one compensation process. In order to solve this problem, we propose a cyclic com-

3.2  Image multi‑scale fusion
In this section, we propose a solution for the defogging of a single underwater microscopic 
image of HABs based on the image Multi-scale fusion principle. Image fusion has been 
widely applied to many applications (e.g., defogging, image synthesis, HDR imaging, and 
multispectral video enhancement) and has demonstrated its utility. In this paper, we propose 
a simple and fast method based on image Multi-scale fusion that can enhance the edge and 
texture features of various underwater microscopic images of HABs. Our framework is built 
based on of a set of input and weight maps derived from cyclic color compensation images. 
Specifically, as shown in Fig. 1, an input image that increases the edge sharpness of the cyclic 
color compensation image and two input images that increase the contrast of the cyclic color 
compensation image are introduced, and the associated weight maps are defined to make the 
edge and texture features of the underwater microscopic images of HABs more significant. 
As shown in Fig. 6, our underwater microscopic image enhancement technique of HABs con-
sists of three steps. First, a color-corrected cyclic color compensation image is obtained from 
the initial underwater microscopic image of HABs. Then, the input image and its associated 
weight map are derived from the cyclic color compensation image. Finally, we fuse the input 
images and their weight maps using a Multi-scale fusion strategy.

3.2.2  Weight maps of input images
By using weight maps in the process of Multi-scale fusion, pixels with high weight values 
in the image can be shown more in the resultant image. We used three types of weight: 
Canny weight, Butterworth weight and Gamma weight.
Canny weight (Wc):The Canny weight map is obtained by applying the Canny opera-
tor on three inputs, which is widely used in medical imaging and image segmentation. 
The canny operator assigns higher values to the edges and thus can segment the edge 
features of an image well. However, this weight will somewhat blur some of the texture 
features of HABs images. To solve this problem, we introduce a second weight, the But-
terworth weight, to enhance the texture features of the image.
Butterworth weight (Wb): The Butterworth weight map is obtained by applying But-
terworth high-pass filtering to the three inputs. The role of Butterworth high weights is 
to enhance the image’s high-frequency information, so the image’s texture features can 
be well preserved after Butterworth high-pass filtering. Because of this, we introduce 
this weight to make up for the deficiency caused by the Canny weight.
Gamma weight (Wg): The first two weights are intended to enhance the edge and tex-
ture features of the HABs images but neglect the enhancement of contrast. Therefore we 
introduce the last weight, gamma weight, to enhance the contrast of HABs images.
In practice, we combine the above three weight maps into one weight map for each 
input. For each input k, we first sum Wc, Wb, and Wg  to obtain Wk. Then we divide the

amid operation, the image loses some high-frequency detail information after convolution

4  Results
In this section, we first validate the cyclic color compensation. Then, the HABs image 
enhancement method in this paper is compared with the existing underwater enhancement

4.1  Cyclic color compensation evaluation
The images used in the experiments were all derived from underwater microscopic 
images of HABs collected in actual seawater. Most of these images have problems such 
as color deviation and blurred texture features. We selected five more classical images 
to evaluate the cyclic color compensation method proposed in this paper. On these five 
images, we applied the following methods: Max RGB, Gray World, Shades of Gray, 
and Gray Edge. To evaluate the effectiveness of our color compensation algorithm, we 
used the evaluation index Qu [ 21], Pearson correlation coefficient (PCC) [1 , 18] and 
the color difference of the image CIE2000. Qu is an index that combines the advan-
tages of the SSIM index and Euclidean color distance, which can effectively evaluate 
the effectiveness of the color compensation method. Qu produces values between [0,1], 
and the higher the value, the better the color compensation. CIE2000 based on the color 
difference formula CIEDE2000 calculated and obtained, CIEDE2000 produces values 
between [0,100], smaller values indicate more minor color differences, and values less

When PCC = 1, the two variables are positively correlated; when PCC = −1, the two

4.2  HABs image enhancement evaluation
The HABs images used in this section are still the five images in the cyclic color com-
pensation evaluation. On these five images, we applied the following image enhance-
ment/restoration methods: UCM [36], ICM [8 ], ULAP [38], RGHS [14], CLAHE [35], 
GC, MIP [25], and Ancuti, et al. [5 ] method. We compare all the image enhancement 
methods mentioned above with our method and use the resultant images produced by all 
enhancement methods and their 3D-RGB color models as a subjective measure of the 
overall color range change of the entire image before and after image enhancement, as 
shown in Fig.  8. We found that the images obtained by our method have a uniform color 
distribution and a broader range of color representation.
To evaluate the effectiveness of enhancement, we used six classical image quality 
evaluation metrics to evaluate the method proposed in this paper. The first parameter is 
the information entropy (Entropy), which is the entropy used to describe the information 
content of an image. The second parameter is the contrast of the image (Contrast). The 
third parameter is the average gradient of the image, which is used to represent the fine 
contrast, texture features and sharpness of the image for the quantitative measurement. 
The fourth and fifth parameters are the enhanced measurement (EME) and the entropy 
of the enhanced measurement (EMEE) to test the output-enhanced images. The sixth 
parameter is the mean structural similarity mean (MSSIM) to measure the similarity of 
the output image to the original image. The value of MSSIM is between 0 and one, and 
closer to one indicates that the resultant image is more similar to the original image.
Figure  9 shows the results of applying several well-known underwater image 
enhancement/restoration methods to degraded HABs microscopic images from our 
HABs dataset. Due to space limitations, we only show five more classical degraded 
images. The experimental results show that the chromatic aberrations of the images pro-
cessed by RGHS, CLAHE, MIP, UCM, ULAP, and Aucuti et al. were not eliminated. 
The images processed by GC and ICM eliminated the color deviation, but the edge and 
texture features were not enhanced (e.g., the flagellar features of HABs did not become 
very clear). In contrast, the method proposed in this paper not only corrects the color 
bias of the underwater microscopic images of HABs but also significantly enhances the 
texture and edge features of the HABs images.
Table  2 shows the quantitative evaluation of the five images in terms of entropy, con-
trast, mean gradient, MSSIM, EMEE, and EME. By observing the experimental results 
in Table  2, the method in this paper has the highest mean values on entropy, contrast, 
mean gradient, EME and EMEE metrics, which indicates that the proposed method in 
this paper can maximize the contrast, edge features, texture features and the best visual 
quality of the images. Meanwhile, we found that the HABs image enhancement method 
proposed in this paper has the lowest value on MSSIM because the value of MSSIM 
is very closely related to the original image. The purpose of image enhancement is to 
improve the contrast of the image and enhance the edge and texture features of HABs 
cells, so the resulting image is bound to have some deviation from the original image. 
The purpose of introducing MSSIM is to detect whether our enhanced HABs images are 
distorted. As shown in Table  2, we found that the MSSIM values of the HABs images 
processed by our method were above 0.75, which indicates that the images processed by 
our method have strong similarities with the original images and do not show distortion. 
In summary, the method proposed in this paper has the best enhancement effect and the 
highest robustness.

5.1  Key points matching
Key point matching is a fundamental task for many image-processing applications. We 
compute key points using the SIFT operator and compare the key point computation and 
matching process of three original images with the images after enhancement utilising the 
method of this paper (see Fig. 10).
Table  3 shows the number of key points in the resulting images obtained by apply -
ing various underwater image enhancement methods using the methods of this paper. As 
shown in Fig.  10, the HABs images processed using our method can detect key points on 
the flagella of HABs, which is impossible with the original images. In summary, our HABs 
image enhancement method can maximize the edge and texture features of the image.
5.2  Edge detection

work, we used the method of Soria et al. [33]. Figure  11 shows the effects of edge detec-

manifestation is that the flagellar features of algal cells become clearly visible) compared 
to the edge detection image of the original image.

of the ROC curve. The larger the AUC, the higher the edge detection accuracy. As shown

5.3  Complexity analysis
In this subsection, we analyze the complexity of the HABs microscopic image enhance-
ment algorithm proposed in this paper. Since the size of the HABs microscopic images 
we use is small and the program takes up only a little memory during operation, space 
complexity is not our primary consideration. We only consider the time complexity of 
the method, so we compared the running time of the image enhancement methods used 
for comparison in the experiments and the proposed method in this paper, respectively, as 
shown in Table 4.
As shown in Table  4, CLAHE, GC and ULAP have a relatively fast runtime, however 
the results in Fig.  9 show that they produce resultant images in which edge and texture 
features are still blurred, which is not what we expect. Also, compared with some image 
enhancement algorithms with the ability to produce competitive results, such as UCM, 
RGHS, MIP and ICM, the runtime for our method is only 13.826 seconds, which is roughly 
similar to the time for RGHS and ICM, about 19 s faster than MIP and about 6 s faster than 
UCM. Overall, the HABs image enhancement algorithm proposed in this paper achieves 
the best enhancement effect and is also competitive in runtime.

6  Conclusion
In this paper, we propose an enhancement method for underwater microscopic images of 
HABs. Our method is based on multi-scale fusion and corrects the chromatic aberration of 
the image by cyclic white balance. Experimental results show that the method proposed in 
this paper can maximize the correction of color deviation of HABs microscopic images and 
enhance the edge and texture features of the images well. In addition, we further demon-
strate the effectiveness of the image enhancement method in this paper using SIFT operator 
and edge detection. We have conducted sufficient experiments on publicly available under-
water image datasets. We found that our method performs better on underwater images 
with large chromatic aberrations, coarse texture features and shallow scattering depths. The 
reader can use the following link to the underwater image dataset for validation: https:// 
github. com/ xahid buffon/ Aweso me_ Under water_ Datas ets. Our future work will further uti-
lize the enhanced HABs microscopic images for classification.
